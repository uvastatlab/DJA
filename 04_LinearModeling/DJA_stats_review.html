The Normal Distribution

The Normal Distribution is basically a curve that looks like a bell. Here’s a picture.

The Normal Distribution

The mean is the hump (red line). The standard deviation is the inflection point, where the curve goes from concave down to concave up (blue lines). It measures spread. Below we see the mean is 0 and the standard deviation is 1.

The Normal Distribution

The mean and standard deviation are like dials or controls on a machine. Changing the mean moves the curve left and right. Changing the standard deviation makes the curve skinny or wide.

The Normal Distribution

Since the mean and standard deviation completely determine the shape of the curve, we call them parameters. Statisticians use the Greek symbols \(\mu\) (mu) to represent the mean and \(\sigma\) (sigma) to represent the standard deviation.

Here’s the formula to draw the line from the Normal distribution Wikipedia article. There is nothing “Normal” about it! It looks pretty terrible! Fortunately, unless you teach a statistics class, you don’t really need to remember it.

The Normal Distribution is a probability distribution

Calling the Normal distribution a probability distribution means the area under the curve is equal to 1, or 100%.

Why do we care about the Normal distribution?

Take a look at this histogram of the heights of 250 randomly sampled US males. Most people are between 65 and 75 inches tall. Does the shape look familiar?

The Normal distribution is a good approximation

If we take the mean and standard deviation of our data sample, we can use those values as parameters in a Normal distribution and draw a smooth curve to approximate the distribution.

Using the Normal distribution

Now we can use the Normal distribution to answer questions such as “what is the probability a randomly selected US male is taller than 6 feet 4 inches (76 inches)?” That’s the area under the curve to the right of 76 inches. Here’s a crude illustration.

Using the Normal distribution

R makes is pretty easy to find the area under the normal curve with the pnorm() function. By default it finds area to the left of the value you give it. To go to the right, specify lower.tail = FALSE. Notice we use the mean() and sd() functions to find the mean and standard deviation of our data.

pnorm(76, mean = mean(heights), sd = sd(heights), lower.tail = FALSE)
## [1] 0.008172683

0.008 rounds to about 0.01, or 0.01 \(\times\) 100 = 1%. It appears there’s about a 1% chance (1 in 100) that a randomly selected US male will be taller than 6 feet 4 inches.

Simulating data from a Normal distribution

Did you really think I randomly sampled 250 US males and got their heights? LOL. Instead I randomly sampled values from a Normal distribution with mean 69 inches and standard deviation 3 inches. I did that in R using the rnorm() function as follows. (The set.seed(1) code ensures you and I generate the same “random” data. Change the 1 to another positive integer and you’ll get another random set of data.)

set.seed(1)
heights <- rnorm(n = 250, mean = 69, sd = 3)

Good to know about the Normal distribution

  • A Normal distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation 1 is called the “Standard Normal Distribution”
  • A shorthand for specifying a Standard Normal distribution is as follows: \(N(0, 1)\)
  • Sometimes Normal distributions are specified with their mean and variance, \(\sigma^2\). Variance is the square of standard deviation.

Myths about the Normal distribution

  • My data should be Normally distributed in order to analyze it. False. Most data is not Normally distributed.
  • Always check to make sure your data is Normally distributed. False. You don’t need to do that.
  • If your data is not Normally distributed you have to transform it to make it Normal. False. Data transformations are common, but that’s not because the data has to be normal.

Then why is the Normal Distribution so important?

Carry out the following thought experiment:

  1. Randomly sample some data, any data, from most any distribution
  2. Calculate the mean
  3. Repeat steps 2 and 3 a million, gazillion times

Those means will be Normally distributed! Kind of crazy, right?

We call that theoretical distribution of the millions of means the sampling distribution.

Show me.

Here’s a Uniform distribution from 1 to 5, denoted \(U(1,5)\). We call it Uniform because every value is equally likely to be distributed. It is not even close to Normal. It looks like a box.

Let’s run a simulation

Take a million samples of size 10 from that Uniform distribution and take the mean of each sample. Then plot the distribution. The replicate() function replicates whatever code we give it. The code in this case takes a random sample of size 10 from a \(U(1,5)\) and then takes the mean. The distribution of means is textbook Normal.

s <- replicate(n = 1e6, mean(runif(n = 10, min = 1, max = 5)))
hist(s, freq = FALSE)
